Music: On the Basis of Sex

Updated: January 6, 2019 By D.Q. Pham

  • Mychael Danna

    Did you know that you could be a composer stationed in a planetarium? I thought that would be one of the loneliest careers, next to reviewing music in your apartment containing only a keytar as an astrophysics bachelor with a knack for music composition. Well, this was the discovery of Mychael Danna, a composer-in-residence. I can relate. While touring with a fraternity brother named Patrick, from the University of Toronto, that was the one flaw I was introduced to in Canada. They closed their McLaughlin Planetarium. The score to immerse the last artificial stargazers was Danna’s “There's No Place Like Home”.

    ⏯

     

    Danna would go on to composing for films from my favourite, (500) Days of Summer to his Oscar-winning, Life of Pi. His latest film was On the Basis of Sex. Initially, I had not a clue that this film existed. The previous year, I had secluded myself with limited films and limited tv-series. I only watched Black Panther, Solo (twice), First Man, and Aquaman as films, with tons of anime within a summer’s month. I was too busy purifying my mind’s output on note progressions and chords. Though Aquaman did make me rethink music differently. The music had a mix of familiarity to Jaws and volcanic music. Personally, I would hope the movie would be phenomenal enough that if the composer were to choose an unrelated genre, it would be beyond memorable. Understandably, it is difficult and the measures would more likely flop.

     

  • Introduction

    Last night, I was in tremendous writer’s block, so I asked what music doctorates are doing. “On the basis of sex,” they said. Okay… Here I go by musical-analyzing this film as they would, but as an inexperienced physics bachelor. I should be a well-versed critic after all the negative reinforcements I was handed for my compositions, but just like it, this will do. Making it spoiler-less is difficult too!

  • Analysis

    First, I have not watched Dennis The Menace since the golden age of VHS recordings of random ABC family movies, but the swaying of the end of some of Danna’s scores at the end of scenes and montages made me recall Dennis The Menace vividly.

     

    More recent nostalgia is the focus on a Colorado man. In the film, Ruth gets into gear on challenging the entire system of gender norms by addressing the Colorado man’s problem of not being able to take care of his sick mother. It turns out, he could not unless he had a family and that was the gender norm of society in the day. The music is contemplative where the violin plays six note progressions with the viola crossing after. Then after the verse, the piano takes over for the viola. I enjoy this cliché where the main character maps out their eventual success with this step-by-step contemplative mood. Then it gets serious as they come to terms about gender again. Danna plays it safe. Which is negative to me, but I will follow up on it further along the analysis.

     

    Fascinatingly, a unique score was when Ruth takes on teaching one of the most difficult degrees. When she paces, the rhythm is that of Hermann’s score from Psycho during the shower scene, except instead of screeching violins, they are major notes. This is quite empowering, and I do not see those rhythms in any other way except in the forefront of a disadvantaged character taking leadership.

     

    When it comes to its classical-ness, as everything as to be prestige when filming Harvard or Columbia, the instruments I could remember are three strings (violin, cello, and viola), hardly piano, and hardly few wind instruments such as the tuba and baritone. Personally, the hardly’s have to go or have to be defined more in key points of On the Basis of Sex. Also, what I would perhaps not do would be to have any hip-hop in the film, but I found out Kesha sings vocal so that I would let that pass.

  • Conclusion

    Overall, I like the “primer” overlaying the entire dialogue. It is not Mychael Danna’s fault that I believe this film shows that its development was rushed. The “primer” is transparent too often because the plot points are too uneven and therefore, the music’s “paint” too. For the music on On the Basis of Sex, I will unintentionally remember Dennis the Menace’s score. It will be forgotten in a week as I continue to stride out of writer’s block. Next many films, give Mychael Danna some time so for all these moviegoers, let them eat Pi!
